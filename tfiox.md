Escaping Rounding Barriers in LLM Quantization via Transverse-Field Ising Optimization Abstract Weight-only post-training quantization (PTQ) pipelines such as AWQ and GPTQ ultimately commit to a deterministic rounding decision for every scalar weight, and recent discrete coordinate-descent quantizers refine those de- cisions one weight at a time. We show that both are blocked by the same obstacle: cor- related activation channels create multi-spin energy barriers in the discrete rounding land- scape — configurations in which no single rounding flip helps, yet a small group of co- ordinated flips strictly reduces the layer recon- struction error. We characterize these barri- ers exactly under the empirical activation met- ric and prove that they arise only from frus- tration between local quantization fields and cross-channel couplings. Building on this anal- ysis we propose Transverse-Field Ising Cor- rection (TFIC) , a quantum-inspired, barrier- aware discrete optimizer that post-processes any AWQ-style pipeline. TFIC casts round- ing as an Ising ground-state problem, assigns each spin an adaptive transverse field com- bining quantization ambiguity, local energy mobility, and frustration, and crosses barriers with tunnelling-inspired cluster moves selected by a barrier synergy score, embedded in an annealed quantum-to-classical schedule that terminates in exact coordinate descent. Ev- ery proposed move is verified against the ex- act reconstruction energy, and a certified spin- fixing condition plus a robust noise-floor ac- ceptance rule guarantee monotone improve- ment that is resistant to calibration-estimation noise. TFIC requires no gradients or archi- tectural changes; preliminary experiments on Mistral-7B and Llama-3-8B show consistent perplexity improvements over AWQ at 4 bits with seconds-per-layer cost and no inference- time overhead. 1 Introduction The deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) at the scale of tens to hundreds of billions of parameters is constrained almost entirely by memory bandwidth and storage. Weight-only post- training quantization (PTQ) has therefore become the dominant compression paradigm, enabling 4- bit and even 3-bit inference with limited quality degradation (Frantar et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024). Modern PTQ pipelines couple two complementary ideas: per-channel weight scaling that migrates difficulty from outlier activations into the weight tensor (Xiao et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024), and second-order or Hessian-aware reconstruction that selects floating-point weights minimizing a local error (Frantar et al., 2023; Nagel et al., 2020). Af- ter these steps, however, every pipeline ultimately performs a deterministic rounding step that snaps each scaled weight to the nearest grid point. This final round-to-nearest (RTN) step is the silent bottleneck of low-bit PTQ. Even with a perfectly chosen scale, the rounding choice for each scalar weight is binary: floor or ceiling. Across a single linear layer with d out × d in weights, this defines a discrete optimization problem with 2 d out d in candidate solutions, and the objective — the squared reconstruction error of the layer output un- der the empirical activation distribution — is in general not minimized by independent per-weight nearest rounding. A diagonal reconstruction met- ric guarantees that independent nearest rounding is globally optimal; for real LLM activations, how- ever, the input Gram matrix has heavy off-diagonal structure produced by correlated channels, and this structure creates the possibility of beneficial coordi- nated rounding decisions among weights that share an output row. Discrete coordinate-descent quantizers (Quan- tEase (Behdin et al., 2023), CDQuant (Nair and Suggala, 2024)) already act on this observation, optimizing the layer objective directly on the grid via single-weight updates with monotone descent. But single-coordinate dynamics have a structural blind spot. We show (§3.3) that correlated chan-

nels generically create rounding barriers : locally stable configurations in which every individual flip increases the reconstruction error, ∆ E j ≥ 0 , ∆ E k ≥ 0 , yet a coordinated pair (or small group) flip strictly decreases it, ∆ E { j,k } < 0 . No sequence of single-coordinate improvements can cross such a barrier, because every intermediate configuration has higher energy. RTN, AdaRound- style projected relaxations, and coordinate-descent quantizers all terminate inside these traps. We argue that the right level of abstraction is dis- crete combinatorial optimization on the rounding lattice , expressed in its natural language: statisti- cal mechanics. Each rounding bit is an Ising spin s ∈ {− 1 , +1 } , the squared reconstruction error is a quadratic energy, and minimizing it is the ground- state problem of a classical Ising model whose couplings are determined by the activation Gram matrix. The barrier phenomenon then has a precise physical reading — it is a classical energy barrier between locally stable spin configurations — and the transverse-field Ising model (TFIM) supplies exactly the missing mechanism: a transverse field acts as a tunneling operator that moves groups of spins through configurations that are classically for- bidden. This is the sense in which our method is quantum-inspired: not because the objective hap- pens to be Ising-like, but because it deliberately searches for correlated transitions across barriers that classical single-spin dynamics cannot cross, while verifying every transition with the exact clas- sical energy. In this paper we propose Transverse-Field Ising Correction (TFIC) , a quantum-inspired, barrier- aware post-processing pass that re-optimizes the rounding decisions made by any standard AWQ- style pipeline. Our contributions are: • Exact Ising formulation. We formulate fixed- grid rounding correction as a classical Ising ground-state problem with exact quadratic cou- plings J = G ⊙ ( hh ⊤ ) under the empirical ac- tivation metric, stated consistently with AWQ per-channel scaling (the Gram matrix is accumu- lated in the scaled activation space, §3.1). • Barrier characterization. We characterize the multi-spin energy barriers that trap nearest round- ing and all single-coordinate discrete optimizers, including an exact pair-flip identity, an explicit barrier instance, and a structural result: under a positive-semidefinite metric, barriers can only arise from frustration between local fields and couplings (§3.3). • Adaptive transverse field. We introduce a per-spin transverse-field score combining quantization-boundary ambiguity, local energy mobility, and frustration, which identifies the “quantum-mobile” spins worth optimizing (§3.4). • Tunnelling-inspired block optimizer. We de- sign a barrier-crossing phase that builds clus- ters by a barrier synergy score S jk = ∆ E j + ∆ E k − ∆ E { j,k } — not by raw coupling magni- tude — and exhaustively optimizes small clusters, embedded in an annealed quantum-to-classical schedule whose final stage is exact single-spin coordinate descent (§3.7). • Certified fixing and exact verification. A dom- inance condition certifies spins whose optimal value is independent of all other spins, every pro- posed move is accepted only under the exact re- construction energy, and a robust noise-floor rule guards acceptance against calibration-estimation noise — together guaranteeing monotone im- provement of the empirical objective (§3.5, §3.8). • Efficient exact implementation. A streaming Gram accumulator, rank-1 RG updates, and a negative result showing that low-rank approxima- tions of G are non-monotone under flip dynamics and must not be used for acceptance decisions (§3.10, Appendix E). By diagnosing the barrier structure of the round- ing landscape and importing the transverse-field toolkit to cross it, TFIC fills the gap between cheap RTN, expensive learned rounding, and barrier-blind coordinate descent, providing a principled correc- tion step that integrates seamlessly into any existing PTQ pipeline. 2 Related Work Post-Training Quantization for LLMs. GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023) performs per- row OBS-style updates that minimize the reconstruction error using inverse-Hessian infor- mation. AWQ (Lin et al., 2024) identifies salient weights from activation magnitudes and protects them via per-channel scaling. SmoothQuant (Xiao

All of these methods terminate in a deterministic RTN step that ignores the cross-channel coupling encoded in the input Gram matrix; TFIC re-optimizes this step. Discrete Coordinate-Descent Quantizers. A re- cent line of work optimizes the layer-wise re- construction objective directly over the quanti- zation grid. QuantEase (Behdin et al., 2023) frames layer-wise PTQ as a discrete-structured non-convex problem and applies cyclic coordinate descent with closed-form per-weight updates and a guaranteed non-increasing objective, together with an outlier-aware variant that is effective in the sub-3-bit regime. CDQuant (Nair and Suggala, 2024) deploys greedy and block coordinate descent as a drop-in replacement for the GPTQ compo- nent inside pipelines such as QuIP and Frame- Quant. COMQ (Zhang et al., 2024) performs backpropagation-free coordinate-wise minimiza- tion for vision transformers, and recent work aug- ments greedy sequential rounding with a bounded search over future rounding decisions (Anonymous (TODO: fill in authors), 2026). TFIC differs from this family in three ways. First, restricting each weight to its two adjacent grid levels yields an ex- act Ising form of the objective, opening the prob- lem to the statistical-mechanics solver toolkit rather than to coordinate updates alone. Second, TFIC ex- plicitly targets the barrier configurations (§3.3) at which single-coordinate dynamics provably termi- nate, and crosses them with correlated multi-spin cluster moves. Third, TFIC is a post-hoc correc- tor : it accepts the committed output of any base quantizer (AWQ scales, and in principle GPTQ or FlatQuant outputs) and refines only the rounding decisions, rather than re-running quantization from scratch. Optimization-Based Rounding. AdaRound (Nagel et al., 2020) relaxes rounding bits to continuous variables in [0 , 1] trained with a sigmoid-based regularizer and rounded only at the end, BRECQ (Li et al., 2021) extends this to block-wise reconstruction with second-order analy- sis, and SignRound (Cheng et al., 2024) replaces the relaxation with signed gradient descent on rounding offsets. These methods deliver strong fidelity but at the cost of many gradient steps per layer. TFIC obtains comparable gains by operating directly on the discrete rounding bits, never leaving the quantization grid, and never invoking gradient-based optimization. From the Ising viewpoint, AdaRound’s continuous relaxation can be read as a mean-field approximation of the same spin energy that TFIC attacks combinatorially; the final projection of a mean-field solution back to the grid offers no protection against the barrier configurations of §3.3. Bias-Correction Methods. Nagel et al. (2019) absorb the expected per-channel quantization error into the bias term of a linear layer. This is incom- patible with bias-less LLM architectures (Touvron et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023) unless new param- eters are introduced. TFIC operates entirely in weight space, so no parameter or kernel changes are required. Ising and Quantum-Inspired Optimization. Mapping combinatorial problems to Ising models has a long history (Lucas, 2014), with quantum and quantum-inspired solvers ranging from quan- tum annealing (Kadowaki and Nishimori, 1998) to simulated bifurcation (Goto et al., 2019). The transverse-field Ising model in particular provides a natural relaxation in which a transverse field Γ permits tunneling between classical configurations, and annealing the field strength interpolates from quantum exploration to classical refinement. To our knowledge, TFIC is the first work to bring this toolkit — adaptive transverse-field spin selec- tion, tunnelling-style cluster moves, and a quantum- to-classical annealing schedule — to PTQ round- ing; prior discrete approaches (Behdin et al., 2023; Nair and Suggala, 2024) remain within single- coordinate update dynamics, which the barrier anal- ysis of §3.3 shows to be structurally incomplete. Hessian-Free Reconstruction. The full Hessian of the layer reconstruction loss is exactly the (scaled-space) Gram matrix G . GPTQ avoids materializing G by working with its inverse via Cholesky updates; we instead materialize G ex- plicitly (only ∼ 64MB at d =4096 ) and exploit its structure for fast incremental updates of RG dur- ing coordinate descent. The trade-off is favorable for transformers, where most linear layers have d ≤ 8192 . Notably, materializing G rather than G − 1 also avoids the noise amplification of inverting an empirically estimated, possibly ill-conditioned matrix, which GPTQ must counteract with diago- nal dampening.

3 Methodology This section formalizes the quantization-correction problem as an Ising ground-state search, charac- terizes the barrier structure of its landscape, and introduces the TFIC algorithm. 3.1 Reconstruction-Error Objective and the Scaled Activation Metric Consider a linear layer with weights W ∈ R d out × d in and calibration activations X ∈ R n × d in collected from n tokens. Following the AWQ paradigm, we apply a per-channel scale c ∈ R d in , C = diag( c ) , chosen to minimize a baseline reconstruction loss; the scaled weights are W sc = W C . The AWQ transformation is an exact reparame- terization of the layer: writing X sc = XC − 1 , the layer output satisfies XW ⊤ = X sc W ⊤ sc . (1) Quantization perturbs W sc on its grid, so the re- construction error of the layer output must be mea- sured against the scaled activations. All activation statistics in this paper therefore live in the scaled space: G = X ⊤ sc X sc n = C − 1 X ⊤ X n C − 1 . (2) Equivalently, one may define the entire residual in the original weight space; the two formulations are identical by (1) . We adopt the scaled-space convention throughout. Quantization on a uniform asymmetric grid maps each scaled weight to one of two adjacent levels, floor ( W sc ) or ceil ( W sc ) . We encode the rounding decision per element as a spin s ij ∈ {− 1 , +1 } via W q, sc ( s ) = M + H ⊙ s, (3) where M is the per-element midpoint between floor and ceil and H is the half-spacing. The de- quantization W q = W q, sc ( s ) C − 1 is a fixed linear map of s for any chosen scale. Fix a row i and a configuration s . For a spin j , let δ j = − 2 s ij H ij be the residual change induced by flipping it; the exact single-flip energy change (derived in §3.6) is ∆ E j = 2 δ j ( RG ) ij + δ 2 j G jj . (10)

Lemma 1 (Pair-flip identity) . For two spins j ̸ = k in the same row, the exact energy change of flipping both simultaneously is ∆ E { j,k } = ∆ E j + ∆ E k + 2 δ j δ k G jk . (11) The cross term 2 δ j δ k G jk is invisible to any opti- mizer that evaluates flips one coordinate at a time. It motivates the central definition of this paper. Definition 1 (Rounding barrier) . A pair { j, k } is a rounding barrier at configuration s if ∆ E j ≥ 0 , ∆ E k ≥ 0 , ∆ E { j,k } < 0 , i.e., neither spin can be flipped individually without increasing the energy, yet flipping both strictly de- creases it. Group barriers over | T | > 2 spins are defined analogously via the group-flip gain (16) . Proposition 1 (Existence and structure of round- ing barriers) . (i) There exist positive-definite Gram matrices G , quantization geometries ( H, D ) , and single-flip-stable configurations s admitting a rounding barrier; an explicit instance with d in = 2 is given in Appendix B. (ii) No sequence of strictly- improving single-spin flips can transform s into the post-barrier configuration: every such path passes through a configuration of strictly higher energy. Corollary 1 (Barriers require frustration) . Since G ⪰ 0 , the quadratic part of (11) is nonnegative: δ 2 j G jj + δ 2 k G kk + 2 δ j δ k G jk ≥ 0 . Hence a barrier requires δ j ( RG ) ij + δ k ( RG ) ik < 0 : at least one spin must sit in a local field that favors its flip while the per-spin quadratic cost for- bids it, and the coupling must release that cost only jointly. Rounding barriers are therefore a manifes- tation of frustration between local fields and cou- plings — they cannot arise from strong couplings alone. Corollary 1 has two algorithmic consequences that shape TFIC. First, the spins worth examining are not merely those near a rounding boundary, but those whose local field is frustrated against their coupling environment — this enters the adaptive transverse field of §3.4. Second, raw coupling mag- nitude | J jk | is the wrong criterion for grouping spins: a strong coupling whose sign agrees with the current configuration contributes nothing to bar- rier crossing. The correct criterion is the barrier synergy S jk := ∆ E j +∆ E k − ∆ E { j,k } = − 2 δ j δ k G jk , (12) which is large and positive exactly when the joint flip is much better than the two isolated flips. Cru- cially, S jk depends on the sign of the coupling relative to the current spin state, and it is free to compute: it requires only G jk and the current con- figuration. 3.4 Adaptive Transverse Field In the transverse-field Ising model, the field strength Γ j assigned to spin j controls its quan- tum mobility — its amplitude for tunneling be- tween classical states. Lemma 1 is the | T | = 2 specialization. 3.7 Annealed Two-Phase Optimizer TFIC organizes its search as an annealed quantum- to-classical schedule. The transverse-field thresh- old follows a decreasing field strength γ (0) > γ (1) > · · · > γ ( A ) , (17) realized as an increasing sequence of pool thresh- olds γ ( a ) th and a decreasing sequence of cluster caps g ( a ) max . At high field (early stages), more spins are mobile and larger, more exploratory clusters are permitted; at low field (late stages), only strongly justified moves survive and the dynamics become increasingly classical. The final stage is exact single-spin coordinate descent — the classical pro- jection. Each stage runs the same two phases: Phase 1: Descend. Column-sequential coordi- nate descent over the current pool drives the con- figuration to a single-flip local minimum (or to the noise floor; §3.8). Two design choices are essential: • Column-sequential, not chunked-parallel. Within a single column, all rows are independent (dif- ferent rows have disjoint spin variables) and may be flipped in parallel. Across columns, how- ever, simultaneously flipping spins j 1 and j 2 of the same row introduces exactly the cross-term 2 δ j 1 δ j 2 G j 1 j 2 of Lemma 1 that the per-column ∆ E does not see. We therefore process columns sequentially in a random permutation, ensuring each flip uses a fresh estimate of R and RG . • Incremental RG maintenance. Naively re- computing RG after each column flip costs O ( d out d 2 in ) per column. Instead we exploit the rank-1 structure of the update: if the column- j flip pattern produces residual update ∆ R : ,j = ∆ s ⊙ H : ,j , then RG ← RG + ∆ R : ,j ⊗ G j, : . (18) This is an O ( d out d in ) outer-product update per column, totaling O ( d out d 2 in ) per sweep — the same cost as recomputing RG once, but amor- tized over all columns. Phase 2: Tunnel. At the resulting local mini- mum, every remaining candidate satisfies ∆ E j ≥ 0 (up to the noise floor); by Proposition 1, all further progress requires crossing barriers. This preserves the monotonicity guarantee un- conditionally while leaving the proposal machinery free to be aggressive. 3.8 Noise-Floor Acceptance The energy E is computed from an estimated Gram matrix. Although G is accumulated over the full calibration set via (5) , the tokens within each cali- bration sequence are strongly autocorrelated, so the effective number of independent samples behind each coupling estimate is far smaller than the raw token count, and individual couplings G jk — and hence individual flip gains ∆ E — carry estimation noise. An optimizer that scans millions of candi- date flips and accepts every ∆ E < 0 is therefore exposed to a selection effect: among the many near- zero candidates, the ones that look most favorable are disproportionately those whose noise happened to favor them, and their empirical gain overstates the true (population) gain. We guard against this with a per-layer robust noise floor. At initialization we compute the flip gain ∆ E of every spin in the pool U and set τ = median ( i,j ) ∈ U ∆ E ij , (19) a robust scale of typical flip-gain magnitude (de- liberately a median, not a standard deviation, to resist heavy-tailed outliers). Descent flips are then accepted only when ∆ E < − κ τ, κ = 2 , (20) i.e., when the empirical decrease clears the noise floor by a margin, rather than merely being negative. Since − κτ < 0 , every accepted flip still strictly de- creases the empirical energy, so the monotonicity and finite-convergence guarantees of the descent phase are preserved a fortiori ; the rule only prunes marginal flips whose benefit is statistically unre- solvable. Cluster moves, whose gains are aggre- gated over multiple coupled spins and are typically far from the floor, retain the strict-descent criterion. Replacing the heuristic constant κ with a quantity derived from the calibration noise level and the number of candidate moves — yielding a formal certificate that accepted flips decrease the popula- tion energy — is the subject of ongoing work. By construction, every accepted flip strictly de- creases E , so each descent phase is monotone and converges in a finite number of sweeps. Empiri- cally 1–2 sweeps suffice per stage once the tun- nelling phase has resolved the correlated structure. 3.9 Initialization Subtleties A surprisingly delicate point in implementation is the initialization s (0) from RTN. Remark 1 (Factorized-residual alternative) . An exact alternative to the Gram form maintains the output-space residual Y = RX ⊤ sc / √ n ∈ R d out × n and updates it columnwise, replacing the O ( d out d 2 in ) per-sweep cost with O ( d out d in n ) . Modern PTQ pipelines effectively spend most of their compute searching over continuous scales and learning continuous rounding bits, then projecting to a discrete grid at the end. TFIC takes the opposite stance: stay on the grid throughout, and import discrete-optimization machinery from statistical mechanics. The resulting algorithm has no learning rate, no convergence threshold to tune, and no risk of the projection step undoing the op- timization. The price is the cost of materializing G , which is acceptable for transformer dimensions (Remark 1 covers the small-budget regime where it is not). Relation to GPTQ. GPTQ also targets the exact reconstruction error and uses second-order infor- mation. The key difference is that GPTQ updates remaining unquantized weights to compensate for already-committed rounding errors, working in continuous weight space and committing one col- umn at a time. TFIC instead re-optimizes already- committed rounding decisions after the fact, work- ing in {− 1 , +1 } space across all columns simul- taneously. The two are complementary: a natural extension is GPTQ followed by TFIC, with the latter mopping up correlated rounding errors that GPTQ’s column-causal ordering cannot address (experiment T3). Relation to coordinate-descent quantizers. QuantEase and CDQuant demonstrate that direct discrete optimization of the layer objective is com- petitive with, and sometimes superior to, GPTQ- style sequential conditioning. TFIC is best read as the next step on that axis along two dimensions. Algorithmically, Proposition 1 shows that single- coordinate dynamics terminate at barrier configu- rations from which strictly better solutions remain reachable only through correlated moves; TFIC’s tunnelling phase is constructed to find exactly those moves, and its synergy score (12) identifies where they are funded. Statistically, TFIC is, to our knowl- edge, the first discrete quantizer to acknowledge that the optimized energy is an estimate : the noise- floor rule of §3.8 prevents the search from harvest- ing flips whose apparent gains are calibration noise, a failure mode to which all exact- ˆ G optimizers — including ours with κ = 0 — are exposed. The transverse field as algorithm, not metaphor. In the TFIM, a transverse field assigns each spin a tunneling amplitude, and annealing the field in- terpolates from quantum exploration to a classical ground state. TFIC instantiates each element of that picture concretely: the classical Ising energy is the exact reconstruction objective (7) ; the transverse field is the adaptive mobility score (13) ; tunneling is the synergy-clustered group flip across the bar- riers of Proposition 1; annealing is the schedule (17) ; and the classical projection is the final exact coordinate-descent stage. Heavier machinery from the same toolkit — path-integral Monte Carlo over the genuine TFIM, or a soft-mode (spin-wave) diag- nostic identifying low-energy collective directions around the converged solution — remains available as future work, but the cluster-exhaustive moves emulate the relevant correlated tunneling events at a fraction of the cost. Approximate proposals, exact acceptance. The design principle of §3.7 deserves emphasis because it resolves a tension that sank an earlier version of this method: speed pressure invites approximating G , but Proposition 3 shows approximate energies are unsafe for acceptance . The resolution is asym- metric — surrogates may nominate moves, only the exact energy may accept them. This keeps the monotonicity guarantee intact while leaving a clear path to scaling the proposal machinery (low-rank sketches, learned proposal models) without revisit- ing correctness.

6 Conclusion We presented TFIC, a quantum-inspired, barrier- aware discrete optimizer that re-optimizes the rounding decisions of any AWQ-style PTQ pipeline. The paper’s central observation is that correlated activation channels create multi-spin en- ergy barriers in the discrete rounding landscape — configurations that trap nearest rounding and every single-coordinate optimizer, yet admit strict im- provement through coordinated flips. TFIC crosses these barriers with an adaptive transverse field that identifies mobile spins, synergy-built clusters that perform tunnelling-style correlated transitions, an annealed quantum-to-classical schedule terminat- ing in exact coordinate descent, and an acceptance discipline (exact energies, certified fixing, noise floor) that guarantees monotone improvement ro- bust to calibration noise. Preliminary results show consistent perplexity gains over AWQ at 4 bits with no inference-time overhead and no architectural changes. The key technical lessons — barriers arise from frustration, not coupling strength alone; cluster moves must be selected by synergy, not magnitude; approximate energies may propose but never accept; and aggressive discrete search must be guarded against calibration-estimation noise — transfer directly to other discrete PTQ optimization problems and are likely to be useful well beyond the specific algorithm proposed here. 7 Limitations TFIC inherits the calibration-distribution depen- dence of all PTQ methods: G is computed from a finite calibration set, and bias toward calibration- domain activations is possible. Although the streaming accumulation (5) lets G absorb every calibration token, those tokens are drawn from a modest number of autocorrelated sequences, so the effective sample size behind each coupling esti- mate is considerably smaller than the raw token count; an optimizer that selects the most favorable among millions of noisy flip gains can therefore still overstate its true improvement (a selection ef- fect). The noise-floor rule of §3.8 is a pragmatic guard against this, but it is a heuristic: it carries no formal guarantee that accepted flips decrease the population reconstruction error, and the mul- tiplier κ is hand-set rather than derived from the calibration noise level and search budget. Devel- oping such a certificate, together with statistically principled shrinkage of the estimated couplings, is ongoing work. On the theory side, Proposition 1 es- tablishes that barriers exist and that the tunnelling phase can cross them, but we do not characterize how prevalent barriers are in real LLM layers — experiment T2 measures this empirically, and a random-matrix analysis of barrier density under realistic Gram spectra would strengthen the story considerably. The components introduced in this version (adaptive field, synergy clustering, anneal- ing, certified fixing) are individually motivated by the barrier analysis but their reported numbers pre- date them; until experiments T1–T7 complete, the empirical section should be read as a feasibility result for the weakest configuration of the method. Beyond these caveats: memory usage scales as d 2 in , which is comfortable for transformer linear lay- ers up to d ≈ 16 , 384 but would require blocking for very wide models; the lm_head layer is not cor- rected by TFIC, leaving room for future integration; and we report results only on AWQ baselines and two model families at 4 bits — broader baselines, the 3-bit regime where coupling effects are larger, and downstream-task evaluations are items T3 and T11.
